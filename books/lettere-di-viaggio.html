The plot of Lettere di viaggio

Genoa, 1492. Christopher Columbus crosses an ocean everyone still calls «the Dark Sea», with three small ships and ninety frightened men. He is looking for Asia; he finds something else — and never fully understands what.

A few years later, a Florentine named Amerigo Vespucci repeats the crossing with different eyes: he measures, he compares, he doubts. And he writes the forbidden sentence: this is not Asia, it is a new world. The real voyages retold from their own letters — the wonder and the courage, and also the chains, and the Taino who had known those islands all along.

Why read Lettere di viaggio to learn Italian

True story, real dates, names you already know: memory does half the comprehension work. The vocabulary of sea and travel is concrete and useful, and the short letter-shaped chapters are a natural model for writing your own first letters in Italian.

Language focus: what you practise

In this level-A2 edition you mainly practise imperfetto versus passato prossimo, the simple future, direct and indirect pronouns. The vocabulary revolves around the sea, travel and discovery: the key words, with translations, are in the glossary below and recur throughout the text, so you learn them in context.

About the author: Colombo e Vespucci

Christopher Columbus (1451–1506), from Genoa, and Amerigo Vespucci (1454–1512), from Florence, described their crossings in letters and logbooks that changed the map of the world — and gave the continent the name of one of them.

Frequently asked questions

What level of Italian do I need to read Lettere di viaggio?

This edition of Lettere di viaggio is rewritten for CEFR level A2. A2 is solid elementary Italian: still simple sentences, but with the imperfetto, the future tense and a wider vocabulary. This reader suits learners with a few months of Italian who want to finish their first complete story.

Is this Colombo e Vespucci's original text?

No, it is an adaptation: the plot and spirit of Colombo e Vespucci's original remain, but vocabulary and syntax are rewritten for level A2, with footnotes, a final glossary and comprehension questions at the end of every chapter.
